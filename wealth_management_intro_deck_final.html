Page 13 / 37 · MBA Tax Paths

Common to all paths — universal moves

  • Order of ops: 401(k) match → HSA → Roth IRA / backdoor → max 401(k) → taxable.
  • Use Roth conversions in low-income years: grad school, sabbatical, pre-revenue startup.
  • Hold > 1 year to move eligible gains from ordinary-income rates into LTCG brackets.
  • Plan state residency before RSUs, deferred comp, or QSBS liquidity.
  • Tax-loss harvest in taxable accounts; carry forward unused losses.
  • Donate appreciated stock to a donor-advised fund around liquidity events.

Retail DCA vehicle for gold exposure

For regular investing, use listed physical gold ETFs or ETCs designed to track spot gold directly, ideally backed by allocated bullion and priced close to the local spot gold benchmark. Examples include GLD, IAU, GLDM, and SGOL.

  • Prefer spot-linked physical gold ETFs/ETCs over gold miners, broad commodity funds, futures funds, leveraged products, or structured notes.
  • Check tracking error, expense ratio, liquidity, bid-ask spread, tax treatment, and whether units are physically backed.
  • DCA can smooth entry timing, but it does not remove gold's volatility or currency exposure.

FIXED INCOME · HOLD TO MATURITY

Take advantage of “hold-to-maturity” protection

Page 21 / 37 · Hold to Maturity
What is hold-to-maturity (YTM)

Buy an individual bond and keep it until the issuer repays principal on the stated maturity date — collecting the scheduled coupons along the way.

Assumptions · the protection holds only if all three are true
1

No credit event like default.

2

No call or prepayment surprise.

3

No forced sale.

The benefit

Under those three conditions, your realized return by holding the bond until maturity is very close to the yield-to-maturity (YTM) quoted at purchase — interim price swings don't change what lands in your account.

→ See Appendix · YTM mechanics for why realized return isn't exactly YTM (reinvestment-rate risk).
Buy this US-Treasury Note at the quoted price and 6.423% YTM is the annualized return you lock in by holding to maturity — provided the three assumptions hold.
Wealth Management Primer · Hold-to-maturity converts price volatility into a cash-flow question.

5.077% is what a 20-year Treasury pays you IF you buy direct and hold to maturity. TLT, which holds the same 20-year Treasuries but never matures, has delivered roughly −13% cumulatively over the past decade. Same underlying bonds; the wrapper is the bet.

Appendix · Fixed Income

Even held to maturity, realized return can undershoot the quoted yield-to-maturity due to reinvestment risk

Page 37 / 37 · Appendix · YTM Mechanics

Why YTM assumes reinvestment at y

YTM is an internal rate of return calculation. It treats every cash flow as if it compounds at one constant rate y.

For a bond priced at P, with periodic coupons C, face value F, maturity n, and YTM y:

Each future cash flow is discounted at the same rate y. Rearranged, the equation implies that if you invest P today and every coupon reinvests at y, your terminal wealth is consistent with earning y annually.

However, the assumption of reinvesting coupons at the same yield y is rarely true in practice.

Worked example — 3-year annual-pay bond

To actually earn the quoted YTM, the first coupon must compound for 2 years, the second for 1 year, and the final coupon arrives at maturity. Terminal value is then:

This equals what your original P would grow to if it compounded at y for 3 years. If coupons reinvest at lower rates, the terminal value is lower — so the realized yield is lower than the original YTM. Higher reinvestment rates push realized yield above YTM.

Key takeaway

Bigger reinvestment-rate range = more reinvestment risk. Zero-coupon bonds eliminate it entirely — no interim cash flows to reinvest, so realized return locks at YTM if held to maturity.
